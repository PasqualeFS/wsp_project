Component 1: Header

Source: Codrops

Target Audience

The Codrops website is targeted toward web developers seeking to increase knowledge in the creation of various web development and design techniques.

Client/User Needs

This header component allows clients to provide users a variety of elements such as brand recognition in the form of a logo or company name, contact information, social network connections and more.

Critique

This particular header for Codrops utilizes two primary colors for the theme, a light blue background with white white text that changes to a darker blue upon hovering. This is nice and gives the user a clear visual of what they're about to select. The blue background offers feelings of stability and integrity while the white gives off feelings of cleanliness and simplicity. The logo is simple and legible located at the top left where most readers will look first being that the eyes read from the top left of the page to right and continue down to the bottom right where the page ends. The user sees the logo first and will move right through the navigation options to the social icons and finally to a light blue magnifying glass indicating a search option. The subtle use of blue bullets between each of the navigation elements help in keeping them equally distanced from one another. There is one font used for the header navigation, Proxima Nova, and it works well combined with the white color keeping the font clean and easy to read.

Reference: http://webdesignledger.com/trends-2/clean-ui-trends-for-large-headers

Component 2: Responsive, Flat Navigation Menu

Source: CSS Menu Maker

Target Audience

Here's a standalone navigation component that probably works well for creative settings, light, whimsical and the little flare out underline as the user hovers can make it appealing to a more youthful audience. Great use for those browsing the web for entertainment websites, blogs and agency-type businesses.

Client/User Needs

A prominent navigation menu that utilizes hover effects to show the user what they're about to select. The solid underline and font-color change on the active state lets them know which page they're currently visiting. A clear and concise navigation menu can help to increase conversions.

Critique

I think it's a simple navigation menu. It's light and easy to code. It does not have a sub-menu option built in so larger, professional websites with a good deal of subpages may want to utilize a different approach to their navigation altogether.

Component 6: Call to Action Component

Source: CSS Deck

Target Audience

The audience for these animated CTA buttons is likely those searching the web for online deals. Consumers looking to buy, download, book a hotel, etc. The default state of this component seems to lend itself to a younger audience that would enjoy flashy, attention getters.

Client/User Needs

The client that uses this may have a need for something extremely noticeable that draws the user in. These animation varieties may be utilized nicely in a variety of marketing needs and themed appropriately to match the overall look and feel of a website. They can also be made to truly stand out like a neon sign. These can assist in increasing user participation and the amount of time spent on a website.

Critique

For you to fully undestand what I'm about to say, you need to click on the source link above and view these CTAs in action. Upon first glance I thought these were incredibly cool simply because they are created with only HTML and CSS. Looking through the code and playing with it in the browser, I find the colors easy enough to modify but I'll have to continue playing around with the animation shapes and direction cycles to truly see how they work. The more time I spent on the website trying to understand them, the more I felt they were annoying me and that they animations should be slowed or made more subtle depending on the client. I would plan to use these sparingly and maybe even not quite as flashy. Too many on a single page of a website would be overkill.

This component is for any and all audiences wanting to know more about (insert company name here) and the team behind what they do. It has a look and feel that will appeal to those who may be science enthusiasts.

Clients gain trust by introducing customers to the talented individuals that make up their team. I exited the video lightbox before it was over and the video disappeared from my browser window but the audio continued to play through until the video was over. Component 9: Rotating Banner Component

Source: WhoIsCarrus.com

Target Audience

The old standard. The rotating banner has applications with a wide variety of audiences. It's big in most cases so it will be noticeable to young and old alike. Medical patients, travelers, bargain hunters and many others will find this display helps point out some major items they should be aware of. It's a large call to action in a sense.

Client/User Needs

I've seen rotating banners used for hospitals, hoteliers, entertainers and more. This is a great way for a client to display roughly two to three items of importance before the user starts scrolling below the fold. By placing links or buttons on the banners it calls the user to click the banner and directs them to the page the client wants them to take notice of. According to one article, eye tracking and A/B testing found that users don't interact with rotating banners. It goes on to even say that they're found to be annoying. However, this still proves to be an easy method for clients to advertise multiple items on a single page and can be more cost effective with regard to time and money spent on development hours.

Critique

Scrolling banners are nice and there are a variety of effects for transitions. They can be made to fade or slide left. Apple made use of a slider some time ago that went in a variety of directions and even sometimes split in the middle. This particular rotating banner is designed by an Orlando agency that I've done some freelance email work for and hope to work with again in the future. Upon entering the site, the banner transitions to new slides on a timer. At the bottom are two circular buttons where the user can manually transition to the next or previous slide without waiting on the timer. It does seem that once the user clicks one of the navigational buttons on the banner, the timer stops and does not transition to any additional slides. I'd like to see if I can reinstate transitions after a user has stopped manually clicking.

Reference: http://northerndiv.com/do-rotating-banners-work/

Component 10: Hoverable Follow/Social Icons

Source: Traveling Children Project

Target Audience

This component can be targeted specifically toward online users young and old seeking to follow a person or company online.

Client/User Needs

For the client, this is a great tool to entice the user toward continuing to see what is happening with them. Whether it's status updates on Facebook, Twitter, reviews on Yelp and Tripadvisor for restaurants and hoteliers, many clients and users will find a user for this component.

Critique

This follow component on Traveling Children Project is themed to match this site for parents and children but the simplicity of it being icons that swap colors upon the user hovering makes it easy to modify depending on the client needs or target audience. While these icons are widely used on nearly every site that I find myself visiting, it's said they distract from the primary call to action and essentially remove a user from your site. I still feel these icons won't be going away any time soon as we continue to progress into a world where the idea of sharing is considered free advertisement. I tend to wonder if we will ever see this trend go away. Until then, most clients want this on their websites, email campaigns and even in printed materials.
